About Mozart

When the genius becomes a man.

His entire childhood is translated into music: his games, his emotions. His sister is also an excellent musician. So the father, proud of his two children, decides to make them known throughout Europe by taking long trips with them. This traveling life, added to the difficult transport conditions of the time, will be detrimental to Wolfgang's already fragile health. Throughout your life you will be affected by different diseases. Another aspect of the childhood of this genius is that, when he came into contact with the world of great personalities so soon, he had practically no opportunity to socialise with other children his age. As an adult, you will have few friends and hardly anyone with whom to share deep ideas. Not even his wife will be able to understand his genius. His travels took him especially to Austria, Germany, Belgium, France, England, Italy. Contacts with these peoples abundantly nurtured their general culture. His music goes beyond all national borders and the limitations they imply: it is not Italian, nor German, nor French. This is what explains the extraordinary universality of Mozart. In 1783 he met the composer Haydn, who was his teacher at first, to become his disciple immediately. They will then be friends and dedicate works to each other. Haydn, a well-known musician in life, will say: "Mozart is the greatest composer in the world today." In 1784 Mozart entered the Viennese Masonic lodge. This contact will have a profound influence on your life and your work. He died in 1791.

The man

Wolfgang Amadeus Mozart possessed the most delicate sense of lyrical poetry, as well as drama. No doubt he must have read a lot: his library contained books on travel, history, and philosophy. Passionate about mathematics, when he was little, he filled tables and walls with numbers. Specially gifted in languages, he knew English, Italian, German, and Latin from liturgical texts. He also appreciated horseback riding, fencing, billiards, dancing. He loved birds: for three years he kept a starling that had learned to sing the first bars of the theme of one of his piano concertos.

His character, his nature

Cheerful by nature, Mozart was fond of entertainment. He loved the things in life, but without being overly attached to them. He was incapable of conceiving evil. There is no sentimentality in his music and he never introduced any vulgar element into it. He never took pity on himself despite his trials. Discreet, his genius will not be revealed in depth other than in his music. He had great love and respect for others. A lover of truth, he was on the lookout for everything he could learn, in a constant search to broaden his understanding. He was ruthless with all forms of oppression. He couldn't bear to be treated like a servant. In his day, aristocrats' contempt for intellectuals and artists was common. He claimed the total independence of the artist, even when it could lead to misery. He bowed before true greatness, and always ignored lowliness, envy. Welcoming to young musicians, it also protected them. When he found someone who was sensitive to his art, he played for hours, even if it was the most insignificant or most unknown man.
